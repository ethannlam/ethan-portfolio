Mini Maker Mill

Project Description

The idea behind the Mini Maker Mill came from frustration with the poor tolerances that come with Fused Filament Fabrication (FFF) 3D printing. While not a big issue when printing models for show, inconsistent tolerances can become a serious issue when printing mechanical components. Currently, the remedy for this issue is manually machining each printed component to fit the required tolerances. This, however, is extremely tedious and time consuming.

To solve this issue I, along with a team of five other engineering students, set out to design an all-in-one solution that automates both the printing and machining processs. This results in both faster and more accurate manufacturing.

The Mini Maker Mill was designed to be compact and space efficient, tailored towards smaller scale operations such as student project teams or startups.

The frame was assembled from slotted extruded aluminum channels and reinforced using aluminum corner brackets. Major components, drill and nozzle mounts were 3D printed with PLA. The drill and nozzle head move along vertical and horizontal lead screws powered by stepper motors.

Autonomous Drone

Project Description

I helped design, build and program an autonomous drone designed to follow a GPS transmitter signal or preselected coordinates. The main goal was to make a multi-purpose drone designed for student interaction on campus. The idea came from the college freshman experience of not knowing where buildings were. The purpose of this drone is to guide students to their whichever building they request.

Getting lost on campus was a feeling the team was all too familiar with, and so multiple different design ideas were considered to remedy this situation. We decided on building a drone instead of a rover, as the rover had the added difficulty of needing to be path dependent, while the drone simply needed to be aware of any obstacles in the y-axis. Building coordinates are predetermined, meaning the main challenge was building the student interface and the autonomous movement.

This project is currently under construction, as we are currently working on finishing both the manual modes and GPS tracking modes. Stay tuned for updates on that, as well as the drone-student interface.

AI Rover Wheel

Project Description

The AI Rover Wheel project focused on the design and implementation of a lightweight, 3D printed wheel for the traversal of a rough, rocky, and unpredictable terrain. The primary goal was to optimaize durability while minimizing weight and cost.

The goal of the AI Rover team was to design and manufacture a rover with a mission to perform soil sample tests in a Mars-like environment. A key component of mission success was the rover's ability to efficiently traverse this terrain. The wheels were designed to work in tandem with a rocker-bogey suspension system.

It was importnat that the wheels were strong enough to support the full weight of the rover, while ramining flexible enough to maintain consistent traction across uneven terrain. This was achieved by using a honeycomb lattice structure, which provided both extra strength and helped retain material flexibility.

The chevron tread design was based off of NASA's Curiosity Rover, and designed to provide traction and stability while preventing any unwanted sideways movement.
